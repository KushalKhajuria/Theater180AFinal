In Sophie Treadwell's Machinal, the Young Woman (Helen Jones) is crushed by a society that operates like a relentless machine. During her trial, the legal proceedings are depicted as a series of rhythmic interruptions. In the modern era, social media has become the new "machine."

Scroll or press the Down Arrow for more

Just as the reporters in Machinal reduced Helen's internal life to sensationalist headlines, TikTok and X algorithms reduced Amber Heard's testimony into smaller, out-of-context clips. This digital machinery doesn't just record what's happening, it turns raw human emotion into a commodity. Because Heard's testimony didn't fit the "perfect victim" script that people expected, the online algorithm treated her like a glitch in the system that needed to be called out.

The Depp v. Heard trial basically turned the courtroom into a reality TV show. This is a lot like the play Machinal, where the setting feels distorted and overwhelming to show how trapped the main character is. However, in the 2022 trial, the distortion didn't come from the stage, it came from the internet. Hashtags like #JusticeForJohnnyDepp acted like a massive, biased crowd that drowned out the actual legal facts. By turning serious testimony into jokes and memes, people weren't just watching a trial, they were performing for their own followers.

The Hero v. Villain Binary

Social media loves to keep things simple: someone has to be the good guy and someone has to be the bad guy. There's no room for a messy victim or a complicated situation. The whole trial was flattened into a Hero vs. Villain story, and since Depp was already a beloved movie star, he got the hero role. This forced Heard into the role of the calculating woman, a stereotype used to discredit women since long before Machinal was even written.

When a woman speaks up against a powerful man in public, she isn't just fighting a court case. She's fighting an old, repetitive script that treats any woman who stands up for herself as suspicious or crazy.

The "Messy" Defendant

One of the biggest reasons the online reaction was so intense is that Amber Heard didn't fit the image of a perfect victim. During the trial, evidence like audio recordings of her admitting to hitting Depp made it clear she wasn't just a passive bystander. This made her a "messy" character in the eyes of the public.

In Machinal, the Young Woman is guilty of a crime, but the play asks us to look at the suffocating world that pushed her there. In the trial, however, the internet had no room for that nuance. As soon as there was evidence that Heard had been toxic or abusive herself, the "machine" decided she was definitely the villain.

People were already suspicious of Heard, so when she showed big emotions on the stand, it backfired. Because she was guilty of being toxic in the relationship, her crying was seen as "bad acting" rather than a human response to a traumatic life.

In the digital world, we want a clear victim and a clear abuser. When evidence showed that both people were probably toxic to each other, people struggled to process it. Instead of seeing a complicated, broken relationship (like the one in Machinal), social media simplified it, using her behavior to completely erase his.

Ultimately, by being a messy character who fought back and had her own share of guilt, she became the perfect target for a digital machine that was already looking for a reason to ruin her.
